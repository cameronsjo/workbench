## Detailed Decision Points

### Decision Point 1: Is the behavior inappropriate and damaging?

**Ask yourself:**
- Does this violate enterprise policy or values?
- Is there material business impact?
- Does it damage team/project/enterprise?
- Is there a pattern of behavior?

**YES if:**
- Clear policy violation
- Documented business impact
- Multiple incidents
- Others are affected negatively

**NO if:**
- Personal preference difference
- Style mismatch
- Single minor incident
- No clear business impact

**Action:**
- **YES:** Continue to next decision point
- **NO:** Monitor situation, document if it recurs

---

### Decision Point 2: Can it be resolved through diplomacy?

**Ask yourself:**
- Have I tried diplomatic approaches?
- Is the person receptive to feedback?
- Could a conversation resolve this?
- Is this their first occurrence?

**YES if:**
- Haven't tried diplomacy yet
- Person is generally reasonable
- First or second occurrence
- They may not realize impact

**NO if:**
- Diplomacy already tried and failed
- Pattern of ignoring feedback
- Actively dismissive or hostile
- Behavior continues despite conversations

**Action:**
- **YES:** Use verbal diplomacy techniques (see communication-styles skill)
- **NO:** Continue to next decision point

---

### Decision Point 3: Do you have sufficient power?

**Assess your power sources:**

**Positional Power:**
- [ ] Role authority
- [ ] Approval rights
- [ ] Veto power
- [ ] Reporting structure

**Resource Power:**
- [ ] Budget control
- [ ] Headcount allocation
- [ ] Asset control
- [ ] Resource distribution

**Expert Power:**
- [ ] Technical expertise
- [ ] Domain knowledge
- [ ] Credibility with leadership
- [ ] Subject matter authority

**Relationship Power:**
- [ ] Network and alliances
- [ ] Trust with leadership
- [ ] Cross-functional relationships
- [ ] Political capital

**Information Power:**
- [ ] Access to critical data
- [ ] Insights leadership needs
- [ ] Documentation of issues
- [ ] Evidence and metrics

**Process Power:**
- [ ] Workflow control
- [ ] Approval gates
- [ ] Policy enforcement
- [ ] Governance oversight

**Sufficient power if:**
- You have at least 2-3 power sources
- Your power directly affects their ability to continue behavior
- You can follow through on consequences
- They depend on something you control

**Insufficient power if:**
- You lack direct leverage
- They can easily work around you
- Your superior would override your actions
- You'd need approval you won't get

**Action:**
- **YES:** Continue to next decision point
- **NO:** Escalate to someone with sufficient power

---

### Decision Point 4: Can you restore them afterward?

**Critical assessment:**

**Ask yourself:**
- Am I willing to genuinely partner with them after?
- Can I let go of resentment?
- Will I hold a grudge?
- Can I provide material restoration?
- Am I committed to rebuilding the relationship?

**YES if:**
- You're acting on principle, not anger
- You believe people can change
- You're willing to partner with them after accountability
- You have concrete restoration actions in mind
- You can genuinely forgive once they accept accountability

**NO if:**
- You're primarily seeking revenge
- You'll hold a grudge regardless
- You can't imagine working with them
- You have no restoration plan
- You're too angry to be objective

⚠️  **CRITICAL WARNING:**
If you cannot genuinely restore them, DO NOT NEUTRALIZE.

**Why this matters:**
Neutralizing without restoration will:
- Damage your reputation
- Create lasting enemy
- Undermine your leadership credibility
- Appear vengeful and petty
- Hurt organizational culture

**Action:**
- **YES:** Continue to risk assessment
- **NO:** Find alternative approach (don't neutralize)

---

### Decision Point 5: Risk and Collateral Damage Assessment

**Assess potential collateral damage:**

**Team Impact:**
- [ ] Will this disrupt team dynamics?
- [ ] Will team members take sides?
- [ ] Will productivity suffer?
- [ ] Will people lose trust in leadership?

**Project Impact:**
- [ ] Will this delay deliverables?
- [ ] Will this affect quality?
- [ ] Will this require rework?
- [ ] Will stakeholders lose confidence?

**Relationship Impact:**
- [ ] Who else will be affected?
- [ ] Will this damage other relationships?
- [ ] Will people see you differently?
- [ ] Will this create factions?

**Reputation Impact:**
- [ ] How will leadership perceive this?
- [ ] How will peers view this?
- [ ] Will this affect your credibility?
- [ ] Will this set unwanted precedent?

**Enterprise Impact:**
- [ ] Will this affect enterprise culture?
- [ ] Will this disrupt other teams?
- [ ] Will this escalate to executives?
- [ ] Will this become a bigger issue?

**Risk levels:**

**Low Risk (Acceptable):**
- Minimal team disruption
- Limited to immediate participants
- Clear business justification
- Strong leadership support
- Easy to contain scope

**Medium Risk (Proceed with Caution):**
- Some team disruption expected
- May affect adjacent teams
- Requires leadership coordination
- Need clear communication plan
- Moderate containment effort

**High Risk (Reconsider):**
- Significant team disruption
- Multi-team impact
- Leadership may not support
- Difficult to contain
- Long-term relationship damage

**Unacceptable Risk (Do Not Proceed):**
- Critical project jeopardy
- Widespread enterprise impact
- Leadership opposition likely
- Cannot contain or manage
- Damage exceeds any benefit

**Action:**
- **Acceptable risk:** Proceed with MOAR framework
- **Unacceptable risk:** Find alternative approach

## Alternative Approaches

If you decide NOT to neutralize, consider these alternatives:

### Alternative 1: Escalation
**When to use:**
- You lack sufficient power
- Risk is too high
- Need organizational backing
- Pattern affects multiple teams

**How to execute:**
- Document situation thoroughly
- Present business impact clearly
- Recommend specific action
- Provide supporting evidence

---

### Alternative 2: Process Changes
**When to use:**
- Systemic issue, not individual
- Process enables bad behavior
- Structural solution is better
- Multiple people exhibit behavior

**How to execute:**
- Identify process gaps
- Propose improvements
- Remove opportunities for bad behavior
- Implement controls

---

### Alternative 3: Accept and Monitor
**When to use:**
- Behavior is decreasing
- Impact is minimal
- Person is retiring/leaving soon
- Cost of action exceeds benefit

**How to execute:**
- Document all incidents
- Set clear boundaries
- Watch for escalation
- Reassess if pattern changes

---

### Alternative 4: Mediation
**When to use:**
- Both sides have valid concerns
- Communication breakdown is primary issue
- Relationship is salvageable
- Neutral party can help

**How to execute:**
- Engage trusted mediator
- Focus on interests, not positions
- Seek mutual understanding
- Commit to specific changes

## Quick Reference: When NOT to Neutralize

❌ **Do NOT neutralize if:**

1. Issue can be resolved through diplomacy
2. You don't have sufficient power to follow through
3. Collateral damage outweighs benefits
4. You're unable or unwilling to restore them afterward
5. You're acting out of anger rather than principle
6. The person is your superior (requires different approach)
7. You lack documentation or evidence
8. Leadership won't support your action
9. You have ulterior motives beyond the stated issue
10. You're making an example of them to warn others

---

## Quick Reference: When TO Neutralize

✅ **DO neutralize when:**

1. Diplomacy has genuinely failed
2. Clear policy or values violation
3. Material business impact
4. You have sufficient power
5. You're willing and able to restore
6. Acting on principle, not emotion
7. Have clear documentation
8. Risks are acceptable
9. Leadership will support (or you have authority)
10. You have concrete restoration plan ready
